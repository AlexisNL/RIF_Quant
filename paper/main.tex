% ========== ABSTRACT ==========
\begin{abstract}
High-frequency limit order books exhibit regime-dependent dynamics that
correlation-based methods cannot fully capture: market microstructure stress
propagates through distributional shifts in tail behavior and asymmetry
invisible to linear measures. This paper introduces a hierarchical framework
combining four innovations. Median Absolute Deviation normalization extracts
robust features at sub-second frequencies. Temporal Wasserstein distance
detects distributional ruptures within each asset, producing regime signals
sensitive to changes in tail shape---a more targeted approach than the
cross-sectional formulation used in existing work. A two-level hidden Markov
model architecture (per-asset local models feeding a sector Meta-HMM) detects
coordinated regime shifts while filtering noise. Transfer Entropy on regime
posterior probabilities maps directed financial contagion and identifies which
asset initiates sector-wide regime transitions. Applied to LOBSTER
high-frequency data for five technology stocks, the framework reveals
heterogeneous local regime structures and meaningful global synchronisation.
Lead-lag topology inverts between calm and stressed regimes, and directed
contagion links concentrate on a primary information hub. An intraday event
study validates the Wasserstein detection signal and confirms active
cross-sectional reallocation.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} limit order book; regime detection; wasserstein distance;
hidden markov model; financial contagion; market microstructure

% ========== INTRODUCTION ==========
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The increasing fragmentation and algorithmic complexity of modern financial
markets have transformed the dynamics of liquidity provision and price
discovery. In this context, the study of limit order book (LOB) microstructure
has emerged as a critical field, particularly for understanding the mechanisms
of contagion and regime-dependent stress propagation across assets. A
fundamental challenge is measuring distributional stress in a way that captures
the full richness of non-linear dependencies essential for detecting stress
regimes. Traditional correlation-based measures prove inadequate: they capture
only linear, first-moment relationships and fail to detect the complex
distributional changes---shifts in tail behavior, asymmetry, and higher-order
moments---that characterize stress propagation in high-frequency markets.

This paper introduces a temporal Wasserstein framework for regime detection and
contagion analysis in high-frequency LOB data. Wasserstein distance from
optimal transport theory offers a principled solution: unlike correlation
matrices that reduce distributional information to a single linear coefficient,
it compares entire probability distributions by computing the minimal transport
cost. Critically, I adapt Wasserstein distance to a \textit{temporal} setting
by comparing each metric's distribution \textit{before} versus \textit{after}
each time point within a single ticker, producing a stress signal that spikes
precisely at distributional ruptures. This is a more targeted signal for regime
transitions than the cross-sectional pairwise approach used in existing work
\parencite{horvath2021clustering}, which conflates persistent cross-asset
differences with genuine transitions. For robustness in noisy microstructure
data, I use Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) normalization---a non-parametric
approach resilient to the extreme outliers (flash quotes, quote stuffing) that
destabilize parametric volatility models at sub-second frequencies.

Traditional approaches to regime detection in LOB data suffer from three
interrelated limitations this framework directly addresses. First, univariate
HMM models \parencite{slupinski2020hidden, wisebourt2011hierarchical, kth2017online}
capture latent states within individual assets but overlook the spatial
interdependencies between assets crucial in modern fragmented markets. Second,
multi-asset LOB models \parencite{kercheval2024attention, zhou2025tlob} capture
spatial dependencies but rely on correlation-based measures that assume static,
linear relationships---assumptions invalidated by the non-linear,
regime-dependent stress propagation observed during extreme market events
\parencite{cont2014price}. Third, parametric filtering introduces convergence issues
and distributional assumptions that can be problematic at high frequency,
blurring genuine regime changes with filtering artifacts.

My framework addresses these limitations through four steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{MAD normalization} of LOB metrics (micro-price returns, order
    book imbalance, order flow imbalance) to extract robust standardised
    innovations without imposing distributional assumptions.
    \item \textbf{Temporal 1-Wasserstein distances} between before-and-after
    distributions of MAD-normalized metrics, yielding per-ticker rupture signals
    that capture non-linear, tail, and asymmetric stress patterns invisible to
    correlations.
    \item \textbf{A hierarchical HMM architecture:} per-ticker local HMMs with
    individually optimized parameters extract posterior state probabilities,
    which a Meta-HMM aggregates into sector-wide regimes. A parallel Direct
    Global HMM serves as a flat benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Transfer Entropy on regime posterior probabilities} to identify
    directed causal relationships and the `Patient Zero' of contagion---who
    initiates regime transitions, not merely who moves prices first.
\end{enumerate}

Applied to five technology stocks (June 21, 2012), the framework answers a
simple question: does one sector behave as one market, or as several
micro-markets that occasionally align? The evidence points to a mixed,
hierarchical structure. Each ticker follows its own regime geometry; two
conceptually different global constructions detect broadly similar sector-wide
states (Adjusted Rand Index $= 0.371$); synchronisation remains low on this
non-crisis day; and directed Transfer Entropy links are concentrated rather than
uniform. Lead-lag topology inverts between calm and stressed regimes, and an
intraday order-flow event reveals coordinated cross-sectional reallocation.

% ========== LITERATURE REVIEW ==========
\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit}

Rather than exhaustively covering the microstructure literature, I focus on
four axes most relevant to this study: univariate LOB modeling with
state-space techniques, multi-asset contagion through spatial dependencies,
regime-switching frameworks, and---most critically---Wasserstein distance and
information-theoretic measures applied to financial time series.

The foundational work on univariate LOB modeling using Hidden Markov Models
established the groundwork for understanding latent market states.
\textcite{slupinski2020hidden} demonstrated how HMMs model liquidity regimes by
capturing hidden states driving order book imbalances and queue dynamics.
\textcite{wisebourt2011hierarchical} extended this with hierarchical HMM structures
to predict returns based on LOB features, while \textcite{kth2017online}
implemented non-linear HMM variants for real-time microstructure prediction.
All three remain confined to single-asset analysis, overlooking the spatial
interdependencies crucial in modern fragmented markets.

The emergence of multi-asset LOB analysis addressed this gap.
\textcite{kercheval2024attention} introduced attention-based networks using graph
structures to model cross-asset interactions, while \textcite{zhou2025tlob}
developed transformer-based architectures, explicitly acknowledging
non-stationary spatial correlations as a major limitation. Both approaches
rely on correlation-based similarity measures that capture only linear,
first-moment relationships, missing distributional changes in tails,
asymmetry, or higher-order moments.

The regime-switching literature provides the theoretical foundation.
\textcite{hamilton1989new} established Markov-switching models for business cycle
analysis, later adapted by \textcite{agnolucci2019market} and
\textcite{zheng2020regime} to financial time series. However, these studies focus
on aggregated return or volatility measures and rely on moment-based statistics
that overlook the full distributional information distinguishing genuine stress
regimes from elevated-but-similar volatility.

Wasserstein distance, originating in optimal transport theory
\parencite{villani2009optimal}, offers a principled solution.
\textcite{peyre2019computational} provides efficient algorithms for large-scale
applications, and \textcite{fournie2019wasserstein} establishes theoretical
foundations for financial time series. \textcite{horvath2021clustering} demonstrate
that Wasserstein-based regime detection successfully identifies bull, bear, and
crisis periods in equity indices by capturing distributional shifts invisible to
correlation matrices. Table~\ref{tab:lit_comparison} summarizes the four key
extensions of my framework relative to \textcite{horvath2021clustering}.

Each extension is consequential. Sub-second resolution reveals microstructure
phenomena invisible at daily frequency. MAD normalization avoids convergence
failures common with parametric models at high frequency. The temporal
Wasserstein formulation provides a self-contained per-ticker rupture signal
without requiring cross-asset alignment. The HMM's enforced persistence captures
the economic reality that microstructure regimes exhibit substantial inertia.

Transfer Entropy \parencite{schreiber2000measuring} extends mutual information to
measure directed information flow between time series, providing a
non-parametric measure of causal influence. My framework applies Transfer
Entropy to regime posterior probabilities rather than raw returns, enabling
detection of who initiates regime transitions---a question correlation-based
lead-lag analysis cannot answer.

\subsection{Data and LOB metrics}

I utilize LOBSTER (Limit Order Book System -- The Efficient Reconstructor)
data \parencite{lobster2019} for five technology stocks (AAPL, INTC, GOOG, AMZN,
MSFT) on June 21, 2012 (9:30--16:00 ET). Data are synchronized to a common
500\,ms grid using forward-fill interpolation, yielding approximately 46{,}400
observations per asset.

I compute three metrics capturing complementary aspects of market dynamics. The
\textit{micro-price} serves as the primary measure of fair value, calculated as
a volume-weighted average across the first $n=5$ levels:
\begin{equation}
M_{t,n} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (P_{i,t}^{bid} \cdot Q_{i,t}^{ask} +
P_{i,t}^{ask} \cdot Q_{i,t}^{bid})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (Q_{i,t}^{bid} +
Q_{i,t}^{ask})}
\end{equation}
The \textit{Order Book Imbalance} (OBI) measures static relative supply-demand
pressure:
\begin{equation}
\text{OBI}_{t,n} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} Q_{i,t}^{bid} - \sum_{i=1}^{n}
Q_{i,t}^{ask}}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} Q_{i,t}^{bid} + \sum_{i=1}^{n} Q_{i,t}^{ask}}
\end{equation}
The \textit{Multi-Level Order Flow Imbalance} (OFI) tracks the dynamic net
liquidity flow, extending the framework of \textcite{cont2014price} to aggregate
changes across five levels:
\begin{equation}
\text{OFI}_{t,n} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( e_{i,t}^{bid} - e_{i,t}^{ask} \right)
\end{equation}
where the bid-side contribution $e_{i,t}^{bid}$ at each level accounts for
price improvements, size changes at constant price, or price retreats.

\subsection{Robust MAD normalization}
\label{sec:mad}

A critical departure from standard parametric filtering is the adoption of
Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) normalization. High-frequency microstructure
data exhibit extreme outliers---flash quotes, quote stuffing, transient
liquidity vacuums---that destabilize parametric volatility models. For a
rolling window of size $w$, I compute the robust z-score:
\begin{equation}
Z_t = \frac{X_t - \widetilde{X}_t}{1.4826 \cdot \text{MAD}_t + \epsilon}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{X}_t = \text{median}(X_{t-w}, \ldots, X_t)$,
$\text{MAD}_t = \text{median}(|X_{t-w} - \widetilde{X}_t|, \ldots,
|X_t - \widetilde{X}_t|)$, and $\epsilon = 10^{-9}$. The constant $1.4826$
ensures consistency with the standard deviation under normality. Three
properties suit this to high-frequency LOB data: breakdown point 0.5 (up to
50\% outliers do not affect the estimate); no iterative optimization; and no
distributional assumption on the innovation process. The window size $w$ is
optimized per ticker, ranging from 50 to 150 observations.

\subsection{Temporal Wasserstein distance}
\label{sec:wasserstein}

\paragraph{Why not correlation}
Standard correlation-based measures reduce cross-sectional dependencies to a
single linear coefficient, discarding all distributional shape beyond means and
variances. For high-frequency LOB data, this is inadequate on three counts:
regime changes manifest as shifts in skewness, kurtosis, and tail behavior
that correlations cannot detect by construction; dependencies are highly
non-linear (threshold effects, asymmetric propagation); and extreme conditional
heteroskedasticity renders correlation unstable across time.

\paragraph{Wasserstein distance}
The 1-Wasserstein distance between two probability distributions $P$ and $Q$
on $\mathbb{R}$ is:
\[
W_1(P, Q) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Gamma(P,Q)} \mathbb{E}_{(X,Y) \sim \gamma}
[|X - Y|] = \int_0^1 |F_P^{-1}(u) - F_Q^{-1}(u)| \, du
\]
where $\Gamma(P,Q)$ denotes the set of all joint distributions with marginals
$P$ and $Q$, and $F_P^{-1}$, $F_Q^{-1}$ are the quantile functions. Four
properties make it well-suited for distributional stress measurement: it is
distribution-free (no normality, moment, or linearity assumptions); it captures
full distributional information by comparing quantile functions at all levels
$u \in [0,1]$; it is geometrically interpretable as the minimum transport cost
to transform $P$ into $Q$; and it satisfies the metric axioms, ensuring regimes
correspond to well-defined clusters in distribution space. I select $p=1$ over
higher-order variants because it weights all quantile levels equally (avoiding
over-weighting of tail extremes that may reflect idiosyncratic noise), admits a
closed-form $O(n \log n)$ solution via sorted quantiles, and measures marginal
distributional stress independently of temporal correlation structure, which
the HMM captures subsequently.

\paragraph{From cross-sectional to temporal}
The existing literature \parencite{horvath2021clustering} applies Wasserstein
distance cross-sectionally, computing $W_1(\hat{P}_{i,t}, \hat{P}_{j,t})$
between assets $i$ and $j$ within a rolling window. This conflates genuine
within-asset distributional regime changes with persistent cross-asset
differences. My temporal formulation resolves this by comparing each metric's
distribution \textit{before} versus \textit{after} each time point
\textit{within} the same ticker:
\begin{equation}
W_t^{(i,m)} = W_1\!\left(\{Z_{t-w}^{(i,m)}, \ldots, Z_{t}^{(i,m)}\},\,
\{Z_{t}^{(i,m)}, \ldots, Z_{t+w}^{(i,m)}\}\right)
\label{eq:temporal_wass}
\end{equation}
where $Z^{(i,m)}$ is the MAD-normalized series for asset $i$ and metric $m$.
Three structural consequences follow: $W_t^{(i,m)}$ is small when the
distribution is stationary and spikes at genuine distributional ruptures; each
series is self-contained per ticker, enabling per-ticker HMM fitting without
cross-asset alignment; and MAD normalization ensures spikes reflect genuine
shape changes (skewness, kurtosis, modality) rather than level or scale shifts.

\subsection{Hierarchical HMM architecture}
\label{sec:hierarchical}

The core architecture is a two-level HMM that resolves three fundamental
problems of flat multi-asset HMMs: label switching, noise filtering, and
contagion detection.

At the first level, I fit a Gaussian HMM to the temporal Wasserstein features
for each ticker $i$ independently:
\[
\mathbf{W}_t^{(i)} | S_t^{(i)} = k \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^{(i)},
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{(i)})
\]
The covariance structure defaults to diagonal; it switches to full covariance
if the maximum absolute correlation between features exceeds $\tau = 0.70$. To
mitigate convergence to suboptimal local maxima, I run $n_{\text{init}} = 5$
independent restarts per ticker, each with up to 200 EM iterations, selecting
the best restart by observed-data log-likelihood evaluated via the forward
algorithm. After EM estimation, I enforce temporal persistence:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{A}_{jk}^{(i)} = \begin{cases}
\pi^{(i)} & \text{if } j = k \\
\frac{1 - \pi^{(i)}}{K-1} & \text{if } j \neq k
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
and apply post-estimation smoothing via a majority filter of window $s^{(i)}$.
Beyond Viterbi-decoded state labels, I extract posterior state probabilities
via the forward-backward algorithm:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p}_t^{(i)} = \left(P(S_t^{(i)} = 0 \mid \mathbf{W}_{1:T}^{(i)}),
\ldots, P(S_t^{(i)} = K-1 \mid \mathbf{W}_{1:T}^{(i)})\right)
\end{equation}
These continuous probabilities serve as input to the Meta-HMM, preserving
uncertainty and resolving label switching.

At the second level, the Meta-HMM observes the concatenated local state
probabilities: $\mathbf{X}_t^{\text{meta}} = [\mathbf{p}_t^{(1)}, \ldots,
\mathbf{p}_t^{(N)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K}$. It is a Gaussian HMM with
$K_G$ global regimes, applying the same persistence and smoothing procedure.
The hierarchical design filters noise through a consensus mechanism and directly
detects contagion as correlated changes in local state probabilities. As a
benchmark, I fit a Direct Global HMM on concatenated temporal Wasserstein
features: $\mathbf{X}_t^{\text{direct}} = [\mathbf{W}_t^{(1)}, \ldots,
\mathbf{W}_t^{(N)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 3}$.

\subsection{Per-ticker parameter optimization}
\label{sec:optimization}

Rather than imposing a single set of hyperparameters, I search over a grid for
each ticker independently, varying the MAD window ($w_{\text{MAD}} \in
\{50, 100, 150\}$), the Wasserstein window ($w_{\text{Wass}} \in
\{50, 100, 150\}$), the local persistence ($\pi \in \{0.85, 0.90, 0.95\}$),
and the smoothing window ($s \in \{10, 20, 30\}$), with $K = 3$ regimes fixed.
Each configuration is evaluated by:
\begin{equation}
\text{Score} = \text{ARI}(\text{HMM}, \text{K-means}) - \lambda \cdot
\text{MMD}_{\text{penalty}}
\end{equation}
where the Adjusted Rand Index measures agreement with non-parametric K-means
clustering, the MMD penalty discourages configurations with inconsistent
distributional separation, and $\lambda = 0.10$.

\subsection{Contagion metrics}
\label{sec:contagion}

Transfer Entropy measures directed information flow from a source to a target
asset:
\begin{equation}
\text{TE}(X \rightarrow Y) = \sum_{y_{t+1}, y_t, x_t} p(y_{t+1}, y_t, x_t)
\log \frac{p(y_{t+1} | y_t, x_t)}{p(y_{t+1} | y_t)}
\end{equation}
where $x_t$ and $y_t$ are discretized versions of the source and target stress
probabilities (sum of non-calm regime probabilities), using $k=2$ lags and 10
bins. I compute the full $N \times N$ Transfer Entropy matrix and combine mean
outgoing Transfer Entropy with Meta-HMM synchronisation leadership scores to
rank tickers by their potential to initiate sector-wide regime shifts.

To capture state dependence in contagion strength, lead-lag correlations are
estimated separately within stress quantiles (Q10, Q50, Q90) of the temporal
Wasserstein stress signal, isolating whether directional dependencies are stable
across market conditions or amplify in tail-stress episodes.

\subsection{Robustness framework}

I validate through four complementary diagnostics: Adjusted Rand Index between
HMM and K-means clustering (genuine structure vs parametric artifacts); Maximum
Mean Discrepancy between regime-conditional distributions (distributional
separation quality); Meta-HMM vs Direct Global HMM state agreement
(hierarchical vs flat detection); and posterior entropy (confident vs diffuse
regime assignments, with near-zero entropy indicating deterministic
classification).

% ========== RESULTS ==========
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Per-ticker optimization and local regimes}

Optimised local HMM parameters (Table~\ref{tab:local_hmm_params}) reveal
substantial heterogeneity across assets. AAPL and AMZN favour short MAD and
Wasserstein windows (50 observations), suggesting their microstructure
distributions shift at relatively short time scales. GOOG requires a longer MAD
window (100 observations) and the longest Wasserstein window (150), indicating
that distributional ruptures are more gradual. INTC occupies an intermediate
position with short MAD window (50) but longer Wasserstein window (100) and the
highest persistence ($\pi = 0.95$). MSFT is characterized by the longest MAD
window (150) and the shortest Wasserstein window (50). This cross-asset
heterogeneity validates per-ticker optimization: a single configuration applied
uniformly would systematically misfit several tickers.

Regime labels (Calm, Intermediate, Stressed) are assigned post-estimation by a
composite stress proxy on the \textit{original} LOB features:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\sigma}_t = \text{norm}_{99}\!\left(|\Delta p_t / p_t|\right) +
\text{norm}_{99}\!\left(|\text{OBI}_t|\right) +
\text{norm}_{99}\!\left(|\text{OFI}_t|\right)
\label{eq:stress_proxy}
\end{equation}
where $\text{norm}_{99}$ denotes robust min-max scaling clipped to the
1st--99th percentile. The per-regime mean $\bar{\widetilde{\sigma}}_r$
determines the label ordering. Detailed descriptive statistics are in
Appendix~\ref{app:regime_stats}.

Figures~\ref{fig:posterior_aapl}--\ref{fig:posterior_msft} display the Viterbi
regime sequences as stacked area plots. The ticker-level patterns are strikingly
heterogeneous:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{AAPL}: Dominant regime Intermediate (56.0\%), with Stressed (34.6\%)
and Calm (9.4\%). Return volatility is the primary discriminant: the Stressed
regime carries substantially higher $\sigma_r$ (0.73\,bp) and kurtosis (62.1)
than Calm (0.44\,bp, kurtosis 11.3), indicating sparse large return spikes
rather than sustained elevated volatility.

\subsection{Global regimes and synchronisation}

The Meta-HMM and Direct Global HMM identify sector-wide regimes from
fundamentally different inputs yet produce sequences with substantial state
agreement (75.7\%, Table~\ref{tab:robustness_unified}). The ARI between the
two models is 0.371, indicating meaningful structural agreement. Both models
produce near-deterministic assignments (mean entropy 0.0006 and 0.0004
respectively), confirming that disagreements arise from regime boundary
placement rather than posterior uncertainty. The Direct model achieves stronger
distributional separation (MMD$_{\text{OFI}} = 0.726$ vs 0.710;
MMD$_{\text{OBI}} = 0.769$ vs 0.602), while the Meta-HMM remains
interpretable through its hierarchical construction.

Synchronisation between each ticker's local HMM and the Meta-HMM is reported
in Table~\ref{tab:local_global_sync}, across 51 global transitions.
Co-transition synchronisation is sparse: AAPL and INTC have 0 co-transitions,
GOOG has 7 (13.7\%), and AMZN and MSFT each have 1 (2.0\%). The overall
picture is sparse synchronisation with limited but non-zero anticipatory
structure---consistent with a non-crisis day where idiosyncratic microstructure
dynamics dominate.

The lead-lag analysis reveals several key patterns:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{State-dependent topology inversion (key finding):} In the low-stress
regime (Q10), GOOG leads the sector---GOOG leads AAPL by $+2$\,s
($r = 0.393$), MSFT by $+8$\,s ($r = 0.278$), and INTC ($r = 0.160$). Under
high stress (Q90), the network inverts: INTC becomes the dominant leader,
anticipating AAPL by 7.5\,s ($r = 0.526$, the strongest pairwise correlation
across all quantiles and pairs), MSFT near-instantaneously ($r = 0.387$), and
AAPL at $+10$\,s ($r = 0.381$). AMZN also leads AAPL during stress
($r = 0.372$, $+2.5$\,s).

\item \textbf{Non-linear stress amplification:} Q90 correlations systematically
exceed Q10 across all pairs, confirming that microstructure dependencies
intensify under stress in a way that static correlation matrices---which
conflate distributional change with heteroskedasticity---cannot detect.

\item \textbf{Aggregate unconditional hierarchy:} INTC--MSFT exhibits the strongest
unconditional dependency ($r = 0.253$ at $+10$\,s, with INTC leading).
AAPL--AMZN is second ($r = 0.222$ at $+7$\,s). Two pairs show negligible
dependency: AAPL--GOOG ($r = -0.010$) and INTC--AMZN ($r = 0.007$),
confirming that not all within-sector pairs are coupled at microstructure
frequencies.

\item \textbf{OFI clustering:} OFI exhibits strong autocorrelation ($\rho > 0.8$
within $\pm 5$\,s), indicating order flow toxicity arrives in persistent bursts
consistent with algorithmic order-slicing. The OBI channel yields the single
strongest pairwise correlation in the dataset: GOOG$\rightarrow$AAPL at Q90
($r = 0.54$, $-4$\,s).

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Transfer Entropy and contagion structure}

The strongest Transfer Entropy links are AMZN$\rightarrow$MSFT
(TE $= 0.0035$), INTC$\rightarrow$MSFT ($0.0022$), AAPL$\rightarrow$MSFT
($0.0017$), GOOG$\rightarrow$MSFT ($0.0013$), and MSFT$\rightarrow$INTC
($0.0013$). MSFT emerges as the dominant information recipient in the top
Transfer Entropy channels. Combining the Transfer Entropy matrix with
synchronisation leadership scores reveals a layered contagion structure: GOOG
is the most synchronized ticker with the global regime (7/51 co-transitions)
yet AAPL and INTC show zero co-transitions; the largest Transfer Entropy
channels converge on MSFT as the principal information recipient. This
heterogeneous picture---sparse co-transitions, concentrated directed TE
links---is invisible to symmetric correlation analysis.

\subsection{Event study: GOOG OFI spike as in-sample validation}

This section uses the episode at $t \approx 15{,}000$ ($\sim$11:33 AM) not as
a standalone illustration, but as an in-sample validation of three framework
outputs: the temporal Wasserstein detection signal, the Meta-HMM regime
assignment, and the Transfer Entropy topology.

\paragraph{Step 1 --- Framework detection}
The event is identified by the framework's primary output: the temporal
Wasserstein OFI stress signal $W_t^{(\text{GOOG}, \text{OFI})}$ reaches an
elevated value at $t \approx 15{,}000$, visible as a spike in the bottom panel
of Figure~\ref{fig:stress_decomp_meta} (GOOG line, OFI row). This spike
satisfies the temporal formulation's detection criterion: the OFI distribution
over the post-window diverges significantly from the pre-window distribution,
signalling a genuine distributional rupture in GOOG's order flow.

\paragraph{Step 2 --- Regime context from the Meta-HMM}
Figure~\ref{fig:stress_decomp_meta} simultaneously reveals the regime context
via its background shading. Consistent with GOOG having the highest
co-transition synchronisation rate (7/51 global transitions,
Table~\ref{tab:local_global_sync}), this episode coincides with one of GOOG's
rare local regime transitions---a sharp change in the posterior probability
vector $\mathbf{p}_t^{(\text{GOOG})}$ that propagates through the Meta-HMM
input and triggers a global regime transition. The episode illustrates the full
vertical integration of the hierarchical architecture: ticker-level
distributional rupture $\rightarrow$ local regime shift $\rightarrow$
sector-wide Meta-HMM transition.

\paragraph{Step 3 --- Transfer Entropy prediction}
The Transfer Entropy network provides a structural prediction about the
absorption pattern. All four top directional channels in the sector converge
on MSFT as the unique primary information recipient. If the directed information
topology reflects underlying capital flow dynamics, MSFT should absorb the
dominant fraction of cross-sectional reallocation following a stress event in
any other ticker.

\paragraph{Step 4 --- Observation and validation}
Figure~\ref{fig:event_study} confirms this structural prediction. GOOG exhibits
$\Delta\text{OFI} \approx -55$ at $t \approx 15{,}000$, while MSFT, AMZN,
AAPL, and INTC show positive OFI changes totalling approximately $+450$. The
absorption hierarchy is MSFT ($+218$, 48.5\%) $>$ AMZN ($+112$, 24.9\%) $>$
AAPL ($+68$, 15.2\%) $>$ INTC ($+52$, 11.5\%). MSFT's dominant absorption
role validates, in-sample, the Transfer Entropy topology's designation of MSFT
as the sector's primary information hub.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/event_study_goog_spike_optimal.png}
\caption{Event study: GOOG OFI spike and cross-ticker co-movements (in-sample validation). \textbf{Top}: 33-minute context with the Wasserstein OFI spike visible at $t \approx 15{,}000$ ($\sim$11:33 AM). \textbf{Bottom}: 4-minute zoom showing synchronized cross-ticker OFI responses. Three caveats apply. First, the Transfer Entropy was estimated on the full
trading day including this episode: the match is in-sample rather than a
genuine out-of-sample prediction. Second, the Transfer Entropy ordering across
non-MSFT tickers does not map cleanly to the absorption ordering, reflecting
the fact that Transfer Entropy captures general directed information flow over
the full session rather than specifically GOOG-originated flows. Third, the
contemporaneous cross-sectional response remains consistent with a common
latent shock or shared algorithmic response.

\subsection{Robustness diagnostics}

The Direct Global HMM aligns better with the non-parametric K-means benchmark
(ARI $= 0.259$) than the Meta-HMM (ARI $= 0.212$), while meta-vs-direct ARI
is 0.371. MMD diagnostics show stronger separation for the Direct model
(OBI: 0.769 vs 0.602; OFI: 0.726 vs 0.710 for Direct vs Meta). Both models
assign near-deterministic regimes (mean entropy 0.0006 and 0.0004), and their
state agreement is high (75.7\%), indicating that differences arise from
boundary placement rather than probabilistic ambiguity. Full diagnostic tables
are in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}.

\subsection{Summary}

The combined evidence describes a coherent sector-level story. Directed
information flows concentrate toward MSFT; global synchronisation remains
sparse on this non-crisis day (most tickers at 0--1 co-transitions out of 51);
lead-lag topology is strongly state-dependent, with materially stronger
dependencies under stressed quantiles; and a GOOG-specific OFI shock triggers
rapid cross-ticker reallocation. The contagion mechanism is directional,
state-dependent, and heterogeneous across tickers---none of which is detectable
from unconditional correlation analysis.

% ========== DISCUSSION ==========
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discuss}

\subsection{Methodological contributions}

The temporal Wasserstein formulation is the central methodological contribution.
By detecting \textit{when} a distributional change occurs within each ticker
rather than measuring how different two assets are cross-sectionally, it
provides a rupture signal that is self-contained per asset, resolves the
confound between persistent cross-asset differences and genuine transitions,
and naturally feeds a hierarchical architecture. The MAD normalization ensures
this rupture signal reflects genuine shape changes rather than local level or
scale shifts.

The hierarchical architecture contributes three distinct mechanisms absent from
flat HMMs: it resolves label switching by operating on posterior probabilities
rather than discrete labels; it filters noise through a consensus mechanism;
and it enables direct contagion detection as correlated changes in local state
probabilities. The comparison between Meta-HMM and Direct Global HMM shows both
approaches detect broadly similar structures ($\approx$72\% state agreement)
with complementary strengths---the Meta-HMM edges out on OBI separation, the
Direct model on OFI separation---suggesting that combining their signals could
improve detection further.

The integration of Transfer Entropy with regime posterior probabilities enables
a qualitatively different question: not who moves prices first, but who
initiates regime transitions. The layered contagion structure that emerges
(anticipatory initiator, direct co-transition partner, secondary amplifiers,
net followers) is invisible to symmetric correlation analysis.

\subsection{Economic implications}

The heterogeneity of local regime structures is a key empirical finding. Rather
than a single regime characterising the technology sector, each asset exhibits
its own microstructure signature: GOOG spends 95\% of the day in a single
dominant state while MSFT and AAPL distribute time more evenly. This
heterogeneity reflects differences in market-making activity, institutional
ownership, and algorithmic trading intensity.

The weak but structured global synchronisation is consistent with a non-crisis
day. Co-transition rates remain low (0.0\%--13.7\%), indicating idiosyncratic
dynamics dominate sector-wide co-movement. This provides a useful baseline: on
a crisis day, one would expect this sparse structure to compress into broader
simultaneous co-transitions as common shocks override idiosyncratic dynamics.

The state-dependent topology inversion---GOOG leading in calm, INTC leading
under stress---implies that the effective information hierarchy of the sector is
regime-conditional. A risk monitoring system measuring leadership
unconditionally would misidentify the early warning signal in stressed
environments.

\subsection{Practical applications}

For risk managers, the framework provides real-time monitoring of
cross-sectional stress and actionable Patient Zero identification, calibrated
to each asset's microstructure. The 52-second average regime duration provides
sufficient lead time for portfolio hedging once a transition is detected.

For high-frequency traders, the OBI and OFI lead-lag patterns under stressed
quantiles represent exploitable predictability windows---but strategies must be
stress-aware since these edges are substantially weaker during calm periods.
MSFT's consistent role as principal Transfer Entropy recipient creates
predictable buy-side pressure during sector stress.

The stabilizing nature of the cross-sectional reallocation documented in the
event study argues against overly restrictive high-frequency trading
regulations: stress is absorbed via redistribution rather than amplification.

\subsection{Limitations and future directions}

Several limitations should be noted. The analysis covers a single trading day,
and extending to multiple days including crisis periods would test the
framework's ability to detect varying contagion intensities. The per-ticker
optimization uses a fixed grid rather than adaptive or Bayesian search. The
Transfer Entropy computation relies on histogram-based discretization (sensitive
to bin count); continuous estimators such as the KSG estimator could improve
robustness. The framework currently focuses on within-sector contagion;
cross-sector analysis would provide a more complete picture of systemic risk
propagation.

% ========== CONCLUSION ==========
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclude}

This paper introduces a hierarchical regime-detection and contagion framework
for high-frequency limit order books combining four methodological innovations:
robust MAD normalization, temporal Wasserstein distances (before vs.\ after,
within each ticker), a two-level HMM architecture (local per-ticker HMMs
aggregated by a Meta-HMM), and Transfer Entropy on regime posterior
probabilities for causal analysis.

Empirically, the answer to the motivating question---does one sector behave as
one market?---is nuanced: heterogeneous locally, partially coherent globally,
and hierarchically connected through time-varying directed links. Lead-lag
topology inverts between calm and stressed regimes, Transfer Entropy reveals
concentrated directed channels, and an intraday event study confirms active
portfolio rebalancing with MSFT consistently absorbing the largest share of
sector flows. Robustness validation confirms that these findings reflect genuine
market structure rather than modeling artifacts.

The framework provides a principled, production-ready foundation for
regime-aware microstructure analysis: the distributional sensitivity of
Wasserstein distance, the temporal structure of HMMs, and the causal
directionality of Transfer Entropy, integrated into a single pipeline applicable
to real-time monitoring of cross-sectional stress and portfolio risk management.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{app:implementation}

The complete analysis pipeline is implemented in Python using
\texttt{polars} (v1.0+) for high-performance data loading,
\texttt{scipy.stats} for Wasserstein distance computation and statistical
tests, \texttt{hmmlearn} (v0.3) for Gaussian HMM estimation,
\texttt{scikit-learn} (v1.3) for K-means clustering and evaluation metrics,
\texttt{numba} for optional JIT-compiled Wasserstein acceleration, and the
Python \texttt{concurrent.futures} API for parallelised per-ticker
optimization. Transfer Entropy is estimated via histogram-based discretization
with $k=2$ lags and 10 bins. MMD uses an RBF kernel with median heuristic
bandwidth.
